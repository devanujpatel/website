Addressing Bias in AI

Before we delve into the regulatory aspect of AI we need to look at a few reasons for bias in AI and the solutions proposed by researchers.

Data used to train AI

One of the primary sources of bias lies in the data used to train AI models, which often reflects historical societal biases and inequalities. Moreover, bias can arise from datasets that lack sufficient representation from minority groups. As a result, the AI generates inaccurate conclusions about those groups. Companies often times use synthetic data to train AI systems when high-quality data is scarce. Which cause further bias in AI.

Researchers suggest using algorithmic aids

Researchers suggest that we be cautious with what data is used to train AI. Companies should be careful while collecting, cleaning and using this data. Any small bias from humans can be amplified by AI. Additionally, researchers suggest using algorithms that help identify and solve imbalance representation from minority groups.

Improper development of AI

AI is a very complex technology. Careless or hasty decisions about how the AI works internally can lead to inaccurate predictions. Mainly this happens due to improper choice of an AI model - something related to how the AI works internally. Another cause for bias in AI is its "black box" nature. refers to the difficulty in understanding how AI arrives at its outcomes, even by its developers.

Intensive testing can help

Researchers suggest testing AI in an isolated, controlled environment called a "sandbox". It involves letting AI make predictions and analysing the results to identify patterns of bias. This can help developers narrow down exact problem areas in the AI.

Diversity issue

The field of CS has extremely lopsided representation of various groups. The field of AI, a subvset of CS, has even worse lopsidedness. Without representation from different backgrounds, experiences, and viewpoints, AI systems may unintentionally reflect the biases of the homogeneous teams that develop them, leading to unfair or discriminatory outcomes.

No apparent solution!

Diverse perspectives are essential for identifying and addressing biases. However, with the current state state of self-regulation in the industry, there is a slim chance companies would make an extra effort to address this issue. THE CURRENT-REGULATORY STATE

Why Self-Regulation is bad?

Self-regulation gives unnecessary freedom to companies. This begets more turmoil than promises. Self-regulation is what is going on in the world right now. Today, women are more likely to be rejected from job applications due to the biased AI systems used in the hiring process. There are countless other instances of bias in AI which unfairly makes marginalized populations more likely to be locked out from jobs, housing or key services. A series of AI fails has shown that a self-regulatory, pro-innovation approach to AI can leave us less safe, without actually making AI better. The fact that companies prioritize winning the “AI Race” over ethical considerations is a blarring red flag. This narrative undermines the notion of self-regulation in the tech industry especially in the highly profiting field of AI.

No accountability

Self-regulation can lack accountability to citizenry. If we leave it up to the individual companies to regulate their own AI, then we will have numerous different ways in which companies would be regulating their AI. There would be a lack of uniformity in accountability measures, potentially leading to inconsistencies and loopholes. This makes it unnecessarily challenging to hold companies accountable for the consequences of their AI systems. Would you like an AI company be unfair to you, and get away with it? This fragmentation undermines public trust, hinders regulatory oversight, and impede efforts to address ethical concerns and societal impacts associated with AI deployment. Another blow is the fact that research has found that employees struggle to have ethics prioritized in a work environment centered around software product launches.

Ignorant and supressive companies

Ney York Times has exposed big tech companies like Google and Microsoft for ignoring their top ethicists while also launching dangerous unifinished AI. They justify there rushed launch saying it wwould be a fatal error to worry about things that can be fixed later. Moreover, Google prevented its top ethicists from publishing their findings on the harmful nature of AI. Furthermore, Microsoft's so called "Office of Responsible A.I." consistently failed to enforce guidelines. Both companies also reportedly disbanded their ethics teams and terminated some employees, possibly to deter others from raising similar concerns. Knowing companies only care about profits and winning the "AI Race", can we still let AI be a self-regulated?

Dire situation ahead

AI is supposed to empower citizens but a recent Pew Research study found that 70% of those surveyed have little to no trust in companies to make responsible decisions regarding AI. Additionally, researchers found 36.8% of ‘facts’ used by AI are biased. To top it off, a study has revealed that companies will run out of high-quality data to train their AI as soon as 2026. Companies will be grasping to build AI but will find themselves unable to do so. Soon this desperation will lead them to covertly employ unethical practices in developing AI like using synthetic data! With such a frightening situation coming up, we can’t have the companies self-regulate themselves because if we do, I wonder what percent of ‘facts’ used to train AI will actually be facts.
