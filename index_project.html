Addressing Bias in AI

Before we dive into the regulatory aspect of AI, we need to look at few of the reasons for bias in AI and the solutions proposed by researchers.

Data used to train AI

One of the primary sources of bias lies in the data used to train AI models, which often reflects historical societal biases and inequalities. Moreover, the AI generates inaccurate conclusions when it is trained on datasets that lack sufficient representation from minority groups. Companies also use synthetic data to train AI systems when high-quality data is scarce which further causes bias in AI.

Researchers suggest using algorithmic aids

Researchers suggest that we be cautious with what data is used to train AI. Companies should be careful while collecting, cleaning and using this data. Any small bias from humans can be amplified by AI. Additionally, researchers suggest using algorithms that help identify and solve imbalanced representation from minority groups.

Improper development of AI

AI is a very complex technology. Careless or hasty decisions about how the AI works internally can lead to inaccurate predictions. Mainly this happens due to improper choice of an AI model. Another cause for bias in AI is its "black box" nature. It refers to the difficulty in understanding how AI arrives at its outcomes, even by its developers.

Intensive testing can help

Researchers suggest testing AI in an isolated, controlled environment called a "sandbox". It involves letting AI make predictions in this sandbox and analysing the results to identify patterns of bias. This can help developers narrow down exact problem areas in the AI.

Diversity issue

The field of CS has extremely lopsided representation of various groups. The field of AI, a subfield in CS, has even worse lopsidedness. Without representation from different backgrounds, AI systems may unintentionally reflect the biases of the homogeneous teams that develop them, leading to unfair or discriminatory outcomes.

No apparent solution!

Diverse perspectives are essential for identifying and addressing biases. However, with the current state of self-regulation in the industry, there is a slim chance companies would make an extra effort to address this issue. THE CURRENT-REGULATORY STATE

Why Self-Regulation is bad?

Self-regulation is what is going on in the world right now. Today, there are countless instances of bias in AI. This unfair and discriminatory nature of AI makes marginalized populations more likely to be locked out from jobs, housing or key services. Bringing more turmoil with it than promies, self-regulation is giving unnecessary freedom to companies. A series of AI fails has shown that a self-regulatory, pro-innovation approach to AI can leave us less safe, without actually making AI better. The fact that companies prioritize winning the “AI Race” over ethical considerations is shocking. This undermines the notion of self-regulation in the tech industry especially in the highly profiting field of AI. Here are some more reasons for why self-reguation is bad:


No accountability

Self-regulation lacks accountability to citizenry. Letting companies self-regulate themselves will lead to thousands of different ways of regulating AI. With a myriad of regulation procedures, there would be a lack of uniformity in accountability measures, potentially leading to inconsistencies and loopholes in how the government penalizes companies. This fragmentation undermines public trust, hinders regulatory oversight, and impede efforts to address ethical concerns and societal impacts associated with AI deployment. Another downside of self-regulation is the fact that employees struggle to have ethics prioritized in a work environment centered around software product launches.

Ignorant and supressive companies

Ney York Times has exposed big tech companies like Google and Microsoft for ignoring their top ethicists and launching dangerous, unfinished AI. They justify their rushed launch saying it would be a fatal error to worry about things that can be fixed later. Moreover, Google prevented its top ethicists from publishing their findings on the harmful nature of AI. Furthermore, Microsoft's so called "Office of Responsible A.I." consistently failed to enforce guidelines. Both companies also reportedly disbanded their ethics teams and terminated some employees, possibly to prevent others from raising similar concerns. Knowing companies only care about profits and winning the "AI Race", we can't let AI be a self-reguated field.

Dire situation ahead

AI is supposed to empower citizens but a recent Pew Research study found that 70% of those surveyed have little to no trust in companies to make responsible decisions regarding AI. Additionally, researchers found 36.8% of ‘facts’ used to train AI are biased. To top it off, a study conducted by Cornell University has revealed that companies will run out of high-quality data to train their AI as soon as 2026. Companies will be grasping to build AI and eventually resort to covertly employing unethical practices in developing AI like using synthetic data! With such a frightening situation coming up, we can’t have the companies self-regulate themselves because if we do, I wonder what percent of ‘facts’ used to train AI will actually be facts.

The AI authority can provide incentives to companies like: more compliant the company is, lesser regulations they get.
